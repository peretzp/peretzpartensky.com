The concept of "the concept concept" is described as elusive and requires further qualifications. It is "a concept propped by a series of assumptions, delivered in a necesserally raw form, practical only partly, and even then in small yeild."

The author explores this through a cactus-based example. The cactus concept yields toothpicks—noting that nature produces them naturally. Mexican omelets contain nopalitos (prickly pear cactus), available at local markets with spikes removed. The author proposes a machine with specific functions: rolling along nopalito surfaces to strip spikes, sorting and selecting longer ones, discarding short spikes and debris, and producing both despined nopalitos and spikes for sale as toothpicks.

This leads to "the cactus martini" concept, combining cactus liquor or substitute, gin or ouzo, and garnished with cactus flesh staked with the aforementioned toothpick.

The author plans to create this cocktail, which would then survive its conceptual status by maintaining all qualifications. However, once documented by a cocktail photographer on a sophisticated urban bar counter and sold as a book on concept cocktails, "it will cease to be."
