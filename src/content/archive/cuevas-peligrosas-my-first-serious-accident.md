One month prior, the author was hanging from a seatbelt in an overturned vehicle on a mountain highway in Baja, Mexico, with blood streaming from their elbow. Now came the time to reflect on the incident.

The author was driving on Highway 1 in Baja, a notoriously dangerous single-lane road marked by frequent "curvas peligrosas" (dangerous curves) warnings. After taking control of the vehicle from a friend near San Quentin, the author approached a blind curve at excessive speed. Unable to navigate the turn, the vehicle swerved toward a steep cliff edge. The author attempted recovery maneuvers, but a rock struck the rear tire, sending the car into multiple rolls.

During the tumble, all windows shattered. The author's elbow struck broken glass and pavement. When the vehicle finally settled upside-down on the opposite lane, the occupants—the author, David, Amanda, and Rupa—assessed their conditions. Remarkably, all four reported being okay, though injuries included cuts and abrasions.

The response proved unconventional. Police arrived and casually instructed the group to drive the destroyed vehicle to town rather than wait for a tow truck. After a jump-start from a stranger, the author's friend David navigated the damaged car 45 miles to Ensenada at minimal speed.

The following morning, the author reflected on the experience with gratitude for their companions' composure and for wearing a seatbelt. Though shaken and unable to drive confidently afterward, the author eventually apologized to their friends and resumed driving.
