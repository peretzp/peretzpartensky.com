The author recounts a harrowing experience during a visit to Moscow while staying with his Cambridge friend Masha Tabak. At a spring party hosted by Sonya, a New York Times correspondent, at a bohemian cafe inside a theater near the Kremlin, the author attended an event populated by American media personnel and Russian intelligentsia.

Due to heavy drinking throughout the evening—a cultural expectation he felt obligated to honor—the author's memory became fragmented. After Masha and her father Yuri departed, the author eventually gained consciousness around 3 a.m., disoriented and realizing he needed to leave. With significantly impaired vision, he located Sonya, who provided 200 rubles and helped him flag down a hired car.

The driver immediately became threatening, demanding money and making vague threats about underpayment. When dropped at an unrecognized metro station, the driver began strangling the author while demanding an additional hundred rubles. Despite his intoxicated state, the author remained remarkably calm, mentally noting his ability to hold his breath from childhood swimming competitions. He mechanically punched the driver in the nose and escaped into the underground station.

After forty minutes of navigation using a map and careful observation, the author found his way home to the Tabak residence, where Masha and her mother awaited anxiously. Upon arrival, he felt "completely sober and happy. Happy to be alive."

The post concludes with practical advice: while hired cars remain useful and economical, passengers should exercise caution in driver selection and sit in back seats to prevent strangulation attempts. The author humorously suggests carrying a gun when possible.
