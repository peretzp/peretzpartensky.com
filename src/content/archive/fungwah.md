A narrative account of a bus journey on FungWah, a budget Chinatown bus service between New York and Boston. The author describes meeting Cassandra, a 27-year-old woman who recently relocated to New York but was returning to Boston to address a crisis.

Cassandra's ex-boyfriend, a Syrian restaurateur from Boston's North End, had stolen her Mercedes after discovering her new address. Police recovered the vehicle, which had been stripped of license plates and fitted with chains and an anti-theft device. The man was in custody awaiting arraignment.

The author explores Cassandra's relationship patterns, noting her attraction to foreign men—including relationships with a Russian and an Albanian. Her mother is Syrian; her German father abandoned the family when she was 14. The author observes that Cassandra seeks structure and order, qualities she associates with her father's parenting style, yet her life remains characterized by recurring crises.

The narrative shifts into a cinematic reimagining of the car theft, describing the ex-boyfriend's journey back to New York with an accomplice. The author advises Cassandra on her legal situation, suggesting a restraining order and plea bargain requiring psychiatric counseling.

When Cassandra receives a phone call, her demeanor changes markedly. The author notes her transformation into "another remote, bitchier person," contrasting with her earlier thoughtful participation in conversation. As the bus arrives in Boston's Chinatown, Cassandra departs alone, her Albanian boyfriend notably absent.
