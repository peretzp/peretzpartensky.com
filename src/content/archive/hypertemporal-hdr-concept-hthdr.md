The author describes a photography project by Matt Goodman at Langton Labs, where a camera was positioned in mountains overlooking Burning Man and triggered every 6 minutes over 5 weeks, capturing 8,000 images. These images were compiled into a time-lapse video titled "Playa Time: Dust to Dust."

The author notes the striking visual differences between nighttime and daytime photography at the location. Where daylight provides uniform illumination revealing the entire landscape, darkness selectively exposes only light sources. To bridge these contrasting states, the author employed Photomatix Pro's HDR (High Dynamic Range) merging capabilities, but applied them across images taken at vastly different times rather than different exposures.

The technique, termed "hypertemporal HDR," blends photographs from different temporal periods. The author demonstrates this method through additional work: merging architectural shots with long exposures of laser light installations created by collaborator Michael Broxton, combining bright work lights with laser illumination against smoke.

The piece concludes by acknowledging similar approaches by other photographers, including Sam Javanrouh's "Slices of the Day" and Martin Krzywinski's "High Dynamic Time Range" (HDTR) tutorial, emphasizing how creative techniques build upon existing methodologies.
