The author shares observations from a seminar on "the modern city." He proposes that historical city walls created spatial constraints that prevented horizontal sprawl, forcing urban centers to develop vertically and with greater complexity—resulting in aesthetically pleasing environments.

After visiting San Francisco following an extended period in Europe, the author noted striking similarities between the two. He hypothesizes that while San Francisco lacks traditional walls, it is constrained on three sides by water and limited vertically by earthquake risk. These natural barriers create the same developmental pressures as historical fortifications, leading to increased urban complexity and a distinctly European character.

The author concludes this represents merely one observation among many possible theoretical frameworks for understanding San Francisco's urban development and character.
