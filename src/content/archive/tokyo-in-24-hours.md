The author writes from a flight to Beijing about a layover in Tokyo that provided cultural preparation before arriving in China. Unlike other immigrants who experienced culture shock all at once, this layover offered a gradual transition.

Upon arrival, the author encountered Japan's famous toilet bidets with electronic sound-masking buttons. Meeting up with fellow EAPSI-China Fellows and friends, the group of six explored Shibuya, Tokyo's vibrant neon district comparable to Times Square.

The author observed Tokyo's efficient infrastructure: trains running with "second precision," compact establishments stacked vertically, and ordering systems using picture-based machines rather than cashiers. A notable cultural practice was the custom of screaming for service at restaurants—"whenever you want something, like beer or a side of squid, you scream."

Through new friendships with locals Tomo and Yuki, the group experienced traditional dining and nightlife at Club Yellow. The author noted peculiar cultural observations: people wearing face masks out of politeness when ill, prolonged eye contact, and pervasive cute characters on signage influencing behavior through emotional appeal rather than authority.

The author sensed "kindness, interconnectedness" alongside a "parallel mythical plane where life is actually conducted," observing both Japanese reserve and emphatic emotional expression throughout the bustling city before returning to Narita Airport.
