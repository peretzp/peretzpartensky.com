The author discusses differing classroom participation norms between England and America. In English classrooms, students tend to remain silent, while American students typically ask questions or contribute to discussion in each class session.

The author proposes that American classroom talk serves to "expose yourself" and test ideas with conviction. When assertions are challenged by contradictory evidence from other students, healthy discussion emerges. This approach works because American college classes replace other communication venues like supervisions, tutors, and informal settings. With limited class hours per week, conversation becomes essential for interaction with professors.

The author then shares a humorous absurdist observation about the 1993 dissolution of Czechoslovakia. The joke suggests that Czech and Slovak leaders named their new countries by drawing a border pen line through a bestselling map showing them as one country. The line supposedly crossed through the letter "o," conveniently dividing the old country name between "Czech" and "Slovakia" as a courtesy to map owners worldwide.
