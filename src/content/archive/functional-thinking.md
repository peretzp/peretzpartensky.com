The author poses a thought experiment about dynamic, function-based laws that encapsulate reasoning rather than remaining static. Two primary examples are offered:

First, retirement age could be calculated as "80% of life expectancy modulated slightly by the needs of the job market as represented by some other metric, and some vague proportionality of the present social security coffers."

Second, suggested jail sentences might be determined through a formula involving crime severity as the primary factor, inverse proportion to incarceration rates for that specific offense, and inverse proportion to overall prison populations, each with different coefficients.

The author suggests such logically structured, dynamic solutions could delay the inevitable need to revisit and reconsider laws. The piece concludes by noting the peculiar futuristic appeal of treating these formulations as tradeable commodities—imagining scenarios where individuals might speculate on derivatives based on retirement ages or jail time calculations.

The post explores how procedural, mathematically-defined legal frameworks might replace traditional static legislative approaches, raising questions about the feasibility and implications of such a system.
