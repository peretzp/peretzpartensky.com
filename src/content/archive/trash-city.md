The author spent summer 2008 in Beijing, living near the Olympic village. While exploring on an electric scooter with a friend named RJ, they discovered a massive recycling operation run by workers on pedal-powered carts loaded with electronics and refuse.

Following these carts through Beijing's streets, they traced the material flow to a neighborhood they dubbed "Trash City." This area functioned as an informal recycling hub with a weigh station at its entrance. Once weighed, loads were distributed through different alleys: "Monitor street branched into Plastic Alley left, Monitor Glass Road to the right."

The neighborhood operated as an open-air disassembly factory where electronics were systematically taken apart. Circuit boards were extracted by young workers using tools, sorted by component type, and transported via diesel trucks. The author noted this contrasted with "cell phone repair" shops elsewhere in the city where skilled technicians carefully soldered components.

Trash City had a vibrant community character. Workers lived where they labored, with communal homes lacking doors and shared facilities. Children played in the streets at night while adults used the space for work by day. Mobile phones coordinated operations and provided light, serving as "their most valuable possessions."

The author and RJ returned multiple times, discovering additional sections including what they called the "Prosthetics Quadrant and Mannequin Circle," where children played with prosthetic limbs as toys.
