Britain maintains a distinctive relationship with its grass, having pioneered significant lawn-based sports including golf, tennis, and football. The first written rules of football were established on Parker's Piece, located near the author's residence in Cambridge.

Britain subsequently exported its lawn passion to America, where suburban culture became centered on verdant lawns. The irrigation of lawns now consumes more water than drinking supplies, according to Harper's Index.

In Cambridge's courtyards, the reverence for grass manifests through permanent "keep off the grass" signage. Those who violate this rule face confrontation from stern English gentlemen who demand "are you a member of this college?" before physically removing offenders.

The college "fellows" possess special privileges permitting them to transgress these restrictions, occasionally "rolling balls around" on the protected lawns while potentially developing another sport for global export.
