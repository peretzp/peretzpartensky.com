The author arrives at a Chinese consulate to obtain a visa, joining a crowd seeking numbers from a ticket machine to determine queue order. Two young Russian security guards—A. (wearing braces) and R. (smoking)—manage the distribution system from behind a glass partition.

After receiving a ticket showing he'd wait approximately one hundred positions, the author converses with guard A. in Russian, asking why Russian youths work at a Chinese consulate. The guard responds simply: "globalization."

The guards then offer assistance, retrieving discarded number stubs from the floor and providing a lower ticket number—roughly a thirty-minute wait instead of hours.

Throughout his time at the consulate, the author observes the guards' behavior with analytical curiosity. They control access to people's time as an "unnatural resource," occasionally pocketing numbers for personal use later—what he terms "queue boosts." These advantages predominantly benefit attractive young women, though elderly women also receive preferential treatment.

The author contemplates their moral framework. Do they view themselves as benevolent dictators or Robin Hood figures prioritizing elderly patrons while indulging in personal gain? He acknowledges benefiting from this "corrupt regime" himself, ultimately concluding that the system's interconnected nature means his beneficiary probably received similarly preferential treatment.
